# Review Scope — Prepared Answer

## The Question

> "What exactly would you like us to review?"

## The Answer

> **The Māori content and semantic representation of the 10 River World stories.**

Specifically:

### 1. Māori Text Accuracy
Are the Māori sentences in each story natural and grammatically correct? There are 10 stories with 49 sentences total. Each has English, Māori, and Afrikaans versions. I want to know if the Māori reads as authentic or if there are translation artefacts.

### 2. Entity Classification
Each word in the stories is linked to a semantic entity type (THING, PERSON, STATE, ACTION, PLACE, PARTICLE, DETERMINER, NEGATION, CONCEPT). Does this typology make sense for te reo Māori? Are there categories missing or categories that don't apply?

### 3. Audio Quality
131 words have audio from Te Aka Māori Dictionary. Are the pronunciations correct? Are there any dialectal issues or mismatches between the written word and the recording?

### 4. Pedagogical Sequence
The stories progress in complexity (level 1 to level 3). Does the progression feel natural for a learner? Are later stories building appropriately on earlier ones?

### 5. Cultural Appropriateness
Are there cultural issues with any of the stories, entities, or examples? The stories are simple (child drinks water, mother gives water, family fishes) but I want to ensure nothing is culturally insensitive.

## What's NOT in Scope (for this review)

- The Afrikaans or English content
- The technical architecture (compiler, planner, prediction ledger)
- The UI/UX design
- Scaling to new stories or languages

## Deliverable

A written review (email or document) with corrections, suggestions, and observations. Can be as brief or detailed as the reviewer prefers.

## Time Estimate

A quick pass through 10 short stories (2–3 paragraphs each) would take approximately 1–2 hours for a fluent Māori speaker.

## If They Want More Depth

> "The system also uses an acquisition planner that models language learning as a state-transition problem — it predicts which missing word will unlock the most sentences. I'd be interested to know whether this computational approach to language acquisition makes sense from a Māori pedagogical perspective, or whether there are aspects of te reo Māori that this model fundamentally cannot capture."
