# Language Revitalisation — Literature Map

**Status:** Outline. Ready for population by reviewer.

## Core question
What do endangered-language projects actually need, and does SRE
address those needs?

## Key takeaways for SRE
1. Revitalisation requires creating speakers, not just archives (Hinton).
   SRE's experience model targets this — it produces interactive learning
   apps, not dictionaries.
2. Community control of data is non-negotiable (Holton, Indigenous Data
   Sovereignty movement). SRE's layered licensing model addresses this.
3. Fishman's GIDS scale classifies endangerment stages. SRE targets
   Stage 7-8 (few speakers, urgent documentation needed).
4. Technology alone cannot reverse language shift — intergenerational
   transmission is the critical factor (Fishman). SRE does not claim
   to replace this.

## Research lineage note
> SRE is situated within the "technology for revitalisation" literature,
> but it makes a specific claim that distinguishes it from prior work:
> that the documentation-to-learning pipeline can be automated via a
> semantic compiler, rather than requiring manual material creation.
> This claim is not yet validated.
